Check Out that Chicken

Is this good for me? ​

  • Naturally low in sodium 
  • 100g of skinless chicken contains 31 grams of protein 
  • Good source of Niacin, Vitamin B6, Vitamin B12, and Biotin

How do I prepare this? 

  • Never rinse poultry before using it because the bacteria can spread everywhere the water splashes creating more of a safety hazard – only cooking will destroy bacteria
  • Separate cooked and raw food products
  • When thawing poultry, place the meat in a container in case juices spill out from the bag
  • Cook poultry immediately after thawing, but you can safely refreeze defrosted poultry if the meat is still cold and ice crystals are still present
  • Cook whole poultry until the thickest part of the breast or thigh is 82C or 180F, do not rely on colour alone to determine if the meat is safe to eat! ​

How do I preserve this?
1 year for a whole chicken at a temperature of less than -18 celsius and 6 months for pieces of chicken
2-3 days in a fridge that is 4 degrees Celsius or lower for both whole chicken and chicken in pieces
​Food Safety
Chicken is a finicky meat and you need to be careful when handling it. Raw Poultry is normally contaminated with Salmonella or Campylobacter bacteria. This sounds scary but can easily be dealt with, so don't let it deter you from that chicken sandwich you've been craving all day. Here are a few basic tips to make sure that you can safely enjoy that chicken:
  • Place poultry in a separate plastic bag to ensure that the juice does not make contact with other foods in the shopping cart
  • Bacteria grow rapidly at room temperature, therefore refrigerate or freeze immediately!
  • Avoid the danger zone of 4°C to 60°C (under 4°C the bacteria are too cold to be active and over 60°C they are inactivated by the heat)
  • Cook fresh poultry within 2 - 3 days after purchasing. If you do not plan on eating the meat soon, freeze it.
  • Separate cooked and raw food products
  • Safest way to thaw frozen raw chicken or turkey is in the refrigerator. Other ways to defrost poultry is in cold water or in the microwave, but never at room temperature

Proper Disposal 

                                                                  Chicken Bones
Chicken bones can be composted in your green bin if your neighbourhood provides one, or thrown into the regular garbage. As many people have discovered, bones should not go in the sink disposal. They will break the blades and bit of meat could end up in the water supply.

                                                                   Spoiled Raw Chicken

​Like any raw meat raw chicken will start to small, attract unwanted pests, and release potentially dangerous bacteria during decomposition. The most practical way to dispose of it is to freeze it in a plastic bag until garbage day and then throw it out. Some cities have programs just for raw meat disposal, so check them out!

                                                                                                   Cooked Chicken 

​Cooked chicken can be disposed of in the same way as raw chicken, by freezing it in a bag and then putting it out on garbage day. Cooked chicken should not be fed to animals as it can make them sick. If possible, why not repurpose it? Try putting chicken scraps into a stew, a sandwich, or a pasta! Less wasted food and money. It's a win for your wallet and the environment.
